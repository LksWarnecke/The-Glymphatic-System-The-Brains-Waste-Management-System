# The Brain's Waste Management: A Summary of the Glymphatic System

> **Note**: This article is a summary of Jessen et al. (2015) and was written as an independent exercise.

## Introduction: The Biology of Central Waste Clearance
The glymphatic system is a waste clearance system engaged in the removal of soluble proteins and metabolites from the central nervous system (CNS). It also helps with the distribution of non-waste compounds like glucose, lipids, amino acids, and more. Although these mechanisms have been observed in experiments by Patricia Grady and Rennels et al. around 1985 (as cited in Jessen et al., 2015) and sparked discussions because the fluid flows could not be explained by the slow process of diffusion, the failure of reproducing these observations led to the incorrect conclusion that the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) is entering the perivascular space only to an insignificant extent (Jessen et al., 2015).

A discussion where the discovery of the glymphatic system may help is the discussion about the reason for sleep. Findings suggest that the glymphatic system mainly functions during sleep and is disengaged during wakefulness, resulting in a significantly higher clearance of neurotoxic waste products during sleep. The dysfunction of the glymphatic system and therefore this clearance function is linked to various diseases, as shown by studies of neurodegenerative disease as well as traumatic brain injuries. In the peripheral tissue, the lymphatic system is a well-known system for removing excess fluid and interstitial solutes. There, the lymph vessel density correlates with the rate of the tissue metabolism. The question arises, how do the brain and spinal cord areas have this disproportionately high metabolic rate, even though there are no lymph vessels present in these areas (Jessen et al., 2015)?

## The Mechanisms of CSF Secretion
A key fluid involved in the activities of the glymphatic system is the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF), which is said to be mainly created through the movement of water and ions, particularly chloride, sodium, and HCO3-, across the epithelial cells at the choroid plexuses. Although there are hypotheses questioning the choroid plexus as the sole production place of CSF (Bulat et al., as cited in Jessen et al., 2015). 

Compared to water, the CSF has a lower protein and potassium concentration,but a higher sodium, chloride, and HCO3- concentration.
Especially important for the CSF production at the choroid plexuses is the Na+/K+-ATPase, creating the transmembrane gradient necessary for ion transport. Another important role is played by the AQP1 channels. Moreover, the CSF production at the choroid plexuses is constant and creates pressure, which dictates the direction of the fluid flow through the ventricular system to the subarachnoid space. The CSF is separated from the blood by the so-called blood-CSF barrier (BCSFB), which is a distinct structure from the blood brain barrier (BBB). The blood-CSF barrier is generally more leaky to water-soluble substances compared to the BBB (Jessen et al., 2015).

## Structural Organization: Brain Vasculature and Perivascular Spaces
Before defining the glymphatic system and having a closer look at it, a general overview of the brain vasculature and perivascular space is helpful to better understand what the glymphatic system actually is and does. Generally, starting from the Circle of Willis, there is an anterior (front) circulation, flowing through the evolutionary younger parts of the brain, including the neocortex, and a posterior (back) circulation, supplying the brainstem and cerebellum. From the Circle of Willis, the blood flows to the surface of the brain, where the vessels are called pial arteries, running through the CSF-containing subarachnoid space.
These pial arteries dive into the brain parenchyma, transitioning into penetrating arterioles. According to the authors, this leads to the creation of a tunnel-like structure surrounding the vessel, creating the so-called perivascular space (also known as the Virchow-Robin space) (Jessen et al., 2015).

The CSF is also driven into the perivascular space through a combination of arterial pulsatility, respiration, and CSF pressure gradients. The pulsation is generated by smooth muscle cells, creating pulse waves along the whole length of the penetrating arteries diving into the brain from the cortical surface. Experiments have shown the importance of pulsation for the CSF-ISF exchange, which plays a key role in the waste clearance mechanism (Iliff et al., 2013, as cited in Jessen et al., 2015).

In the brain parenchyma, the vasculature are surrounded by astrocytic endfeet, creating the outer wall of the perivascular space, resulting in a “donut-shaped tunnel” around the vasculature. Along deeper penetration into the brain parenchyma, the Virchow-Robin Spaces become smaller and finally disappear, becoming continuous with the basal lamina. The basal lamina is very porous and has almost no resistance, which enables the CSF to easily leak out of the pipes into the brain tissue. This leakage also happens in states where the perivascular space still appears (Jessen et al., 2015).

This CSF influx into the brain parenchyma is done through AQP4 water channels, which are highly concentrated in the astrocytic endfeet in a healthy human. This continuous influx drives interstitial fluid fluxes within the tissue toward perivenous spaces surrounding the large deep veins, causing the ISF to be collected in the perivenous space, from where it drains out of the brain towards the lymphatic system, taking metabolites along with it, washing them out of the brain. Used blood and fluid move into cortical veins, which are inside the brain and travel back to the surface, finally becoming pial veins, dumping the fluid into large sinuses, and sending it back to the body for filtering.

This highly polarized macroscopic system dealing with the convective fluid fluxes with rapid interchange of CSF and ISF is called the glymphatic system. The name arose because of the functional similarities to the lymphatic system and the importance of the glial AQP4 channels in the convective fluid transport of the glymphatic system (Jessen et al., 2015).

##Evidence for CSF Movement and Clearance Function
Over the years, many experimental studies have been performed to analyze the functioning of the brain's clearance system. The status quo suggests that there is convective movement of periarterial CSF into and through the brain's parenchyma and that this facilitates the clearance of interstitial solutes to perivenous drainage pathways. There are, for example, experiments showing the clearance of beta-amyloid from the brain by the glymphatic system in mice (Iliff et al., 2012, as cited in Jessen et al., 2015). Moreover, there have been experiments knocking out the AQP4 channels in mice, leading to a reduction of CSF fluid flux through the parenchyma compared to wild-type control mice by ~65%. In the same experiments, a reduction of the clearance of intrastriatally injected beta-amyloid by ~55% was noticed, underlining the importance of the glymphatic system as a major pathway for clearing interstitial fluid solutes from the brain's parenchyma. Weller and colleagues (as cited in Jessen et al., 2015) proposed that the failure of this clearance system of interstitial solutes may be linked to neurodegenerative diseases, including Alzheimer’s disease, which is characterized by the accumulation of proteins, including amyloid plaques and tau tangles (Jessen et al., 2015).

##The Glymphatic Role in Lipid Distribution
The blood-brain barrier prevents the influx of lipids (including, for example, cholesterol) and lipoproteins into the brain. So, the brain has to create the necessary lipids and lipoproteins itself. Lipoproteins, like Apolipoprotein E and J, which are released by astrocytes and produced at the same location as the CSF, the choroid plexuses, are not only important for the clearing of excess hydroxylated cholesterol and beta-amyloid but are also very important for the delivery of lipids (like cholesterol) to the neurons in the brain. Apolipoprotein E allele 4 is mentioned, for example, as a major risk factor for Alzheimer’s disease because it is disrupting these mechanisms. The glymphatic system is therefore the pathway for clearing metabolites and, on the same end, also for distributing important lipids in the brain (Jessen et al., 2015).

##Sleep as a Driver of Metabolic Waste Removal
The reason for sleep is a long-discussed topic; the authors state that sleep has to have some evolutionary benefit because it is a vulnerable state, and animals wouldn’t put themselves into this position without gaining benefits. One possible explanation is the important role of sleep for memory consolidation, which could be a benefit against other species (Buzsáki, 1998, as cited in Jessen et al., 2015). Pure energy saving is probably not a main cause, since metabolism is only reduced by ~25% during sleep (Madsen et al., 1991, as cited in Jessen et al., 2015). 

With the discovery that the glymphatic system is more active during sleep compared to the wakeful state, a new argument regarding this discussion has been established. The higher activity during sleep results in enhanced clearance of neurotoxic waste products, which are produced during wakefulness. The neurotransmitter norepinephrine, which is a major arousal driver and present during wakefulness, is said to be suppressing the glymphatic system and production of CSF while the individual is awake (Jessen et al., 2015).

##Reduced Glymphatic Activity During Aging
During aging, the activity of the glymphatic system is reduced, which the authors mention could be caused by dysregulation of astroglial water transport as well as a decline in CSF production and CSF pressure. Aging is said to be one of the major risk factors for neurodegenerative diseases; this aligns with the knowledge that the clearance mechanism of the glymphatic system is reduced during aging, which causes a higher accumulation of misfolded and hyperphosphorylated proteins, making the brain more vulnerable to developing neurodegenerative pathology. One key substance in this is beta-amyloid, which is produced by cells (including oligodendrocytes and neurons) as a waste product and whose production is increased especially during wakefulness. This extracellular beta-amyloid is quickly produced and also cleared in young, healthy humans. Here, it appears that the clearance primarily happens through the glymphatic system. Failure or reduced functioning of the glymphatic system therefore causes accumulation of beta-amyloid. The fact that the anatomical routes of interstitial bulk flow in the perivascular space are consistent with sites of beta-amyloid accumulation supports this hypothesis (Jessen et al., 2015).

##Traumatic Brain Injury and the Escalation of Neurodegenerative Risk
Traumatic brain injuries can increase the risk of Alzheimer’s disease because those injuries induce the release of beta-amyloid peptides and of C-tau (Pop et al., 2013; Zemlan et al., 2002, as cited in Jessen et al., 2015), as well as cause the formation of large astroglial scars and AQP4 channel mis-location, similar to what is happening during aging. Iliff et al. (2014, as cited in Jessen et al., 2015) showed that CSF-mediated removal of tau via the glymphatic routes is crucial for limiting secondary neuronal damage following traumatic brain injury. The C-tau hypothesis, which describes a downward spiral of tau causing attraction of more tau, which leads to the formation of neurofibrillary tangles, resulting in “prion-like” spread of pathology (Guo & Lee, 2011; Jucker & Walker, 2011, as cited in Jessen et al., 2015), is consistent with this finding. The impairment of the glymphatic system can cause additional risk factors because normal metabolic waste cannot be transported away from the brain, which suggests that the degree of the impairment of the glymphatic system after a traumatic brain injury might contribute to variability in the outcome (Jessen et al., 2015).

##Concluding Summary
The discovery of the glymphatic system provides a long-awaited answer to how the brain manages its high metabolic demands without traditional lymphatic vessels. Through the pathway of the glymphatic system, the CNS is able to effectively purge neurotoxic waste products like beta-amyloid while simultaneously distributing essential nutrients like lipids and glucose. This process relies on a balance of arterial pulsatility and the unique physiology of the sleep state.

Understanding the glymphatic system might be helpful for addressing the challenges of the aging brain. As the system declines over time or is impaired by acute trauma, the resulting accumulation of proteins creates a direct link to neurodegenerative pathology. Continued research into these fluid dynamics, assisted by modern imaging techniques, may soon offer new diagnostic pathways and therapeutic targets for preserving cognitive health across the lifespan (Jessen et al., 2015).
